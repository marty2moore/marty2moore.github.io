Lone Peak

July 2024  ·  Salt Lake City, Utah

← Back to Trip Reports

Day 1

The approach to Lone Peak via Jacob's Ladder was pretty demanding; we tracked roughly 7 miles one way with over 6,000 feet of elevation gain. We hiked in with all the gear needed to camp AND climb... Our packs weighed roughly 60-70 lbs each. The route begins following a gradual dirt road before joining the Jacob's Ladder ridge around mile 2. Above the treeline, the trail opens onto white granite slabs with Lone Peak looming ahead. A series of Class 2–3 scrambles leads through the southwest cirque to the summit at 11,253 feet.

On the first day, Steven, Nate and I hiked in, set up camp, and napped. The hike destroyed us. We set off from the trailhead around 6am, to avoid the 90+ degree summer heat, and struggled up the elevation. Our only source of water for the week was a small snow melt stream near camp. We ended up setting up camp on a lower section of the mountain for the first night, and would end up moving to a higher section the next day.

Nate and I with the cirque in the background. We have a long way to go. I switched shirts to protect myself from the sun.

Night 1 campsite. We will later move up much closer to the wall.

Day 2 - The Open Book

The Open Book is a 4-pitch, 5.7+ trad climb on the Summit Wall of Lone Peak Cirque and one of the great alpine routes in the Wasatch. The line follows a sweeping dihedral system up roughly 600 feet of high-quality granite, with a variety of climbing along the way: polished lower-angle slabs, off-width cracks, chimneys, and twin cracks. What makes it special is the finish: the route tops out directly on the summit of Lone Peak. The rating feels stout for the grade.

On the second day we all swapped leads on The Open Book. On the approach, we had to problem solve to cross a large section of snow. This was an excellent route with great exposure. This was also the first time on the trip where I realized how much the elevation was impacting me; I was breathing hard as I climbed and it was harder to recover during rests.

Shaving out footholds in the snow so we can cross over and start the route.

On route.

After finishing the route, we were on top of the world.

Day 3 - Lowe Route, 5.8

On my 25th birthday, we climbed the Lowe Route. We hiked up Pete's Staircase and set up the ropes for Lowe Route. The Lowe Route is a 3-pitch, 5.8 trad climb on Question Mark Wall in Lone Peak Cirque, widely regarded as the best moderate route on the wall. The first pitch is the highlight: a clean hand crack in a dihedral that leads to a ledge belay marked by two old pitons. The second pitch wanders at 5.7, and the third brings you to the top of the wall with the full cirque spread out below.

Steven led the first, Nate the second, and here I am leading the 3rd.

That night, we had the most insane sunset to celebrate my 25th, I look back on this day as one of, if not, THE best day of my life. One direction, we could see for miles out towards civilization, and the other was the Cirque.

Day 4

A few friends hiked in to meet us on the third day; for the fourth day, we weren't planning on climbing in teams of 3. Nate and I planned on climbing the extremely chossy West Ridge, 5.6. On our approach, we found a fun 3-pitch "shortcut" to take instead of hiking around and up. This route lower route was not documented anywhere and it turned out to be pretty high quality and a perfect challenge for us. As we were linking this first climb with the base of the West Ridge, we were having a hard time with loose rock. We reached the base of the climb and had to set up a belay anchor to protect the belayer.

Nate was about 40 feet up the first pitch (4th of the day) when we decided it was best to bail. He found a nut, ensured it was solid and I lowered him back down to me. Steven and Jason were also bailing on Triple O's. We were all pretty worked. Jason and I decided to run up the Open Book again.<\p>

With a slower party of 3 in front (our other friends), and a late start to the day. We ended up being on the wall for ~8 hours... This was a brutal climb filled with a lot of waiting. We reached the peak just as the sun was setting. Luckily Nate and Steven were hiking up the back side of the mountain with rescue gear since we were taking so long. They had extra headlamps, water, and snacks. It was a beautiful night, but I was too stressed to properly enjoy it since we had to hike out early the next morning to catch a flight.

Day 5

Getting ready to pack up in a bug filled camp. As soon as I got home from this trip, I bought a bug net for the next one.

The hike out was an absolute sufferfest. We were worked from the week and our packs were just as heavy as the hike in. We made it out and caught an Uber home to shower before our flights. I feel bad for our Uber driver; none of us had showered in 5 days.
